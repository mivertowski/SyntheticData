\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

The digitization of enterprise resource planning (ERP) systems has produced
vast repositories of financial transaction data that underpin modern
accounting, auditing, and regulatory compliance.  Machine-learning approaches
to fraud detection~\citep{west2016intelligent,hilal2022financial},
continuous auditing~\citep{vasarhelyi2004principles}, and anomaly
analytics~\citep{schreyer2017detection} have shown considerable promise,
yet they face a persistent data-access challenge: real accounting data is
proprietary, regulated under frameworks such as SOX~\citep{sox2002} and
GDPR~\citep{gdpr2016}, and rarely available for open research.

Synthetic data generation offers a path forward, but existing approaches fall
short in several dimensions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Statistical fidelity.}  General-purpose tabular generators
    (GANs, VAEs, CTGAN~\citep{xu2019modeling}) do not enforce domain-specific
    invariants such as balanced double-entry bookkeeping, Benford's-law
    compliance~\citep{benford1938law,nigrini2012benfords}, or accounting
    equation coherence ($A = L + E$).

  \item \textbf{Relational completeness.}  Enterprise data is inherently
    multi-table and multi-process.  A purchase order triggers goods receipts,
    vendor invoices, and payments; these in turn affect subledgers, trial
    balances, and financial statements.  Generating tables in isolation
    destroys these referential chains.

  \item \textbf{Anomaly realism.}  Injecting fraud and error patterns post hoc
    often produces anomalies that are trivially detectable or statistically
    implausible, limiting the usefulness of generated data for training robust
    detection models.

  \item \textbf{Privacy guarantees.}  When real data characteristics must be
    preserved, na\"ive summary statistics may leak sensitive information.
    Formal privacy mechanisms are needed to bridge the gap between real-data
    fidelity and confidentiality.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We present \system{}, a comprehensive open-source framework that addresses
all four challenges simultaneously.  Our contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item A \textbf{layered generation architecture} (\Cref{sec:architecture})
    comprising 15 Rust crates that separate domain models, statistical
    distributions, business-process generators, output sinks, and evaluation
    into composable modules.

  \item A \textbf{statistically grounded sampling engine}
    (\Cref{sec:statistical_foundations}) featuring log-normal mixture models,
    five copula families for dependency modeling, and first- and second-digit
    Benford compliance with provable mean-absolute-deviation bounds.

  \item A \textbf{multi-process generation pipeline}
    (\Cref{sec:generation_pipeline}) that produces coherent data spanning
    Procure-to-Pay (P2P), Order-to-Cash (O2C), Record-to-Report (R2R),
    Source-to-Contract (S2C), hire-to-retire (HR), and manufacturing processes,
    with three-way matching, intercompany elimination, and period-close
    procedures.

  \item A \textbf{multi-stage anomaly injection framework}
    (\Cref{sec:anomaly_injection}) with 33 anomaly types, progressive fraud
    schemes, temporal clustering, cascade propagation, difficulty scoring, and
    ground-truth labels suitable for supervised and semi-supervised learning.

  \item A \textbf{privacy-preserving fingerprint module}
    (\Cref{sec:privacy}) that extracts distributional profiles under
    $\varepsilon$-differential privacy and $k$-anonymity, enabling
    generation of synthetic data that mirrors real-world characteristics
    without exposing individual records.

  \item A \textbf{comprehensive evaluation suite}
    (\Cref{sec:evaluation}) with 15 coherence validators, statistical
    goodness-of-fit tests, ML-readiness metrics, and an auto-tuning engine
    that generates configuration patches from evaluation gaps.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Scope and audience.}
\system{} targets three communities: (a)~ML researchers needing realistic
labeled accounting datasets for fraud detection and anomaly analytics,
(b)~auditors and regulators seeking synthetic test environments for continuous
auditing tools, and (c)~ERP vendors requiring high-volume test data for system
integration and stress testing.
